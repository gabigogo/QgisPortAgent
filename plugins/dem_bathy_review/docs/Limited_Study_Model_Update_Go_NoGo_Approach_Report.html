DEM Comparison Methodology Report

WPT 2.0 - Limited Study Channel Topography Update Decision Framework for Hydraulic Model Refinement

1. Executive Summary

This methodology describes the systematic approach used to compare two Digital Elevation Models (DEMs) to determine whether updated topographic data should be incorporated into existing hydraulic models. The process uses a go/no-go decision framework applied to individual drainage channels, evaluating the statistical significance of elevation differences between the 2022 Level of Service (LOS) Limited Study Topography (LOS DEM) and the updated MAAPNext Topography (MAAPNext DEM).

Primary Objective Identify channels where topographic changes are statistically significant enough to warrant updating the hydraulic model geometry, ensuring efficient resource allocation and model accuracy improvements.

2. Input Data Sources

2.1 Topographic Datasets

The comparison utilizes two distinct topographic datasets representing the same geographic area at different points in time or derived from different data collection methodologies:

  • DEM A – LOS DEM (2022 Level of Service Limited Study Topography): Represents the baseline topographic surface currently used in the existing hydraulic models. This dataset reflects channel conditions from the 2022 County-wide Level of Service study.
  • DEM B – MAAPNext DEM (MAAPNext Topography): Represents updated topographic data developed as part of the MAAPNext program, incorporating Atlas 14 rainfall data and potentially newer survey information. This dataset may reflect recent channel modifications, erosion, sedimentation, or improved data collection techniques.

2.2 Channel Centerline Layer

A vector GIS layer containing polyline features representing the centerlines of drainage channels within the study area. Each channel feature includes a unique identifier and optional grouping attributes (e.g., watershed designation, unit number, or jurisdictional area). The centerlines define the spatial extent and organization of the comparison analysis.

3. Analysis Parameters

The methodology employs standardized parameters to ensure consistency and reproducibility across all channel comparisons. These parameters control the spatial sampling strategy and statistical thresholds for decision-making.

The 0.5-foot bias threshold represents a practical minimum difference that would meaningfully impact hydraulic model results, while the 12-foot outlier tolerance filters data quality issues without removing legitimate topographic variations.

4. Detailed Methodology

4.1 Spatial Sampling Strategy

For each channel in the centerline layer, the analysis follows a systematic spatial sampling approach to characterize elevation differences across the channel corridor:

1

Corridor Delineation

The channel centerline is buffered by 30 feet on both sides (60-foot total corridor width) using a flat cap and round join style. This creates a uniform analysis zone that follows the channel alignment while maintaining consistent lateral extent.

2

Sample Point Generation

A regular grid of sample points is generated at 5-foot spacing within the buffered corridor polygon. The grid alignment is optimized to maximize point coverage while maintaining spatial regularity. Typical channels generate hundreds to thousands of sample points depending on length.

3

Elevation Extraction

At each sample point location, elevation values are extracted from both the LOS DEM ($z_A$) and the MAAPNext DEM ($z_B$) using bilinear interpolation. Points falling in NoData regions of either DEM are flagged and tracked separately but excluded from statistical calculations.

4

Difference Calculation

For each valid sample point, the elevation difference is computed using the convention:

$$\Delta z = z_A - z_B = z_{\text{LOS}} - z_{\text{MAAPNext}}$$

Positive values indicate the LOS DEM is higher than MAAPNext DEM (potential channel incision or MAAPNext showing deeper channel). Negative values indicate the opposite (potential aggradation or MAAPNext showing shallower channel).

4.2 Outlier Exclusion Process

To ensure robust statistical characterization unaffected by data quality issues or localized anomalies, an outlier exclusion protocol is applied:

  1. Absolute Difference Test: Each sample point difference is evaluated against the 12-foot outlier tolerance threshold using the criterion: $|\Delta z| \leq 12.0$ feet
  2. Exclusion Flagging: Points exceeding the threshold are flagged as is_excluded = 1 in the output dataset and counted separately from valid samples
  3. Statistical Impact: Excluded points are not used in computing:
    • Mean bias (average difference)
    • Root Mean Square Error (RMSE)
    • Standard deviation
    • Median difference
  4. Minimum Sample Requirement: After outlier exclusion, a minimum of 3 valid sample points must remain for the channel to be included in the analysis. Channels failing this criterion are flagged with a warning and excluded from decision-making.
Important Note on Min/Max Values Minimum and maximum difference values are computed from all valid points (including outliers) to preserve visibility of the full observed range. This distinction ensures extreme values are documented even when excluded from central tendency metrics.

4.3 Statistical Metrics Computation

Following outlier exclusion, the remaining "clean" dataset of elevation differences is used to compute descriptive statistics for each channel:

4.3.1 Bias (Mean Difference)

$$\text{Bias} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Delta z_i$$

Represents the systematic average offset between the two DEMs. Positive bias indicates LOS DEM consistently higher; negative bias indicates MAAPNext DEM consistently higher.

4.3.2 Root Mean Square Error (RMSE)

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\Delta z_i)^2}$$

Quantifies the overall magnitude of differences, incorporating both systematic bias and random variability. RMSE is always non-negative and sensitive to large deviations.

4.3.3 Standard Deviation

$$\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\Delta z_i - \text{Bias})^2}$$

Measures the variability of differences around the mean bias, indicating consistency of the offset across the channel corridor.

4.3.4 Median Difference

The 50th percentile value of the difference distribution, providing a robust central tendency measure less sensitive to remaining extreme values than the mean.

4.4 Grouped Summary Analysis

When channels are organized into groups (e.g., by watershed, unit number, or jurisdictional area), the methodology computes group-level summary statistics by aggregating all valid sample points across all channels within each group. This provides higher-level decision support for watershed-scale planning and prioritization.

Group summaries include all the same statistical metrics as channel-level results, plus:

  • Total number of channels in the group
  • Total number of valid sample points across all channels
  • Total number of excluded outlier points
  • Count and proportion of channels flagged as "significant"

5. Go/No-Go Decision Framework

The primary output of this methodology is a binary recommendation for each channel regarding whether the hydraulic model topography should be updated using the MAAPNext DEM. The decision is based on statistical significance of the observed bias.

5.1 Decision Criterion

$$\text{GO} \iff |\text{Bias}| > 0.5 \text{ feet}$$
$$\text{NO-GO} \iff |\text{Bias}| \leq 0.5 \text{ feet}$$

5.2 Decision Matrix

Condition Mathematical Test Recommendation Interpretation
Significant Deviation Detected $|\text{Bias}| > 0.5$ ft GO Strong evidence for topographic change. Update hydraulic model geometry with MAAPNext DEM to improve model accuracy and representativeness.
No Significant Deviation $|\text{Bias}| \leq 0.5$ ft NO-GO Lack of evidence for meaningful topographic change. Existing LOS DEM adequately represents current conditions; model update not warranted at this time.

5.3 Decision Rationale

The 0.5-foot bias threshold represents a practical engineering judgment balancing several considerations:

  • Hydraulic Sensitivity: Elevation changes of 0.5 feet or greater typically produce measurable impacts on water surface elevations, flow velocities, and flood extent delineation in 1D steady-state hydraulic models
  • Data Accuracy: The threshold exceeds typical DEM vertical accuracy specifications (commonly ±0.3 to ±0.5 feet for LiDAR-derived products), reducing false positives from data uncertainty
  • Operational Efficiency: The threshold filters channels with trivial differences, focusing model update resources on areas with substantive topographic changes
  • Regulatory Relevance: The threshold aligns with typical floodplain mapping vertical accuracy standards and regulatory reporting precision

6. Quality Assurance and Limitations

6.1 Data Quality Considerations

  • Coordinate System Consistency: All datasets are projected to a common coordinate reference system (CRS) before analysis to ensure spatial alignment
  • Temporal Consistency: Users should verify that DEM collection dates are appropriate for the comparison objective and that no major channel modifications occurred between collections
  • NoData Handling: Areas with missing elevation data in either DEM are excluded from statistics but documented in coverage metrics
  • Edge Effects: Buffered corridors near channel endpoints or lateral boundaries may exhibit reduced sample density

6.2 Methodological Limitations

  • The analysis assumes DEMs are already in vertical datum alignment; systematic vertical datum differences will appear as bias and may lead to incorrect GO recommendations
  • The 12-foot outlier threshold may remove legitimate topographic features in channels with significant vertical drops (e.g., grade control structures, waterfalls)
  • The methodology does not account for seasonal or environmental factors affecting DEM collection (e.g., vegetation, water surface presence)
  • Statistical significance does not inherently indicate which DEM is more accurate; supplementary ground-truth data or survey information may be needed for validation

6.3 Validation Steps

  1. Review spatial distribution of excluded outlier points for patterns indicating data quality issues
  2. Compare min/max values against known channel characteristics to identify anomalous results
  3. Visually inspect sample points overlaid on DEMs in areas flagged as significant
  4. Consider running sensitivity analyses with alternative threshold values for critical channels

7. Conclusion

This methodology provides a systematic, statistically-grounded framework for making efficient and defensible decisions about hydraulic model topography updates. By comparing the LOS DEM and MAAPNext DEM at high spatial resolution and applying consistent significance criteria, the approach identifies channels where topographic changes materially impact model accuracy while filtering those where differences are negligible.

The go/no-go decision framework enables resource-efficient model maintenance by focusing update efforts on areas with substantive change, while the comprehensive documentation and QA outputs support technical defensibility.
